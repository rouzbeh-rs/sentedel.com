Closing the NTE Gap in EDI PHI Detection

The first model trained to handle PHI detection across both structured X12 segments and embedded NTE free text — and the benchmark that proves it works.

The Problem

The segment nobody was talking about

Every X12 EDI de-identification tool — rule-based or ML-based — handles the structured segments well enough. Names in NM1, addresses in N3/N4, dates of birth in DMG: these sit in predictable positions with known qualifiers, and a well-configured system can mask them reliably.

But there's a segment that breaks the pattern entirely: NTE.

The NTE segment is a free-text field embedded inside an otherwise structured transaction. In an 837P professional claim, NTE appears in Loop 2300 (claim-level notes) and Loop 2400 (service line notes), and it can contain anything a clinician or billing specialist decides to type. Prior authorization notes with patient names and dates of birth. Referral notes with provider names and facility names. Administrative corrections with member IDs and claim numbers. All written in natural language, not in predictable element positions.

Industry feedback

"Look at how the model fares against PHI data in the NTE segment — that's where most of these solutions lag."

Results

What we found

We benchmarked four models on 500 synthetic 837P transactions, 162 of which contained NTE segments with PHI embedded in clinical free text. The NTE content was generated using Qwen 2.5 7B-Instruct to produce diverse, realistic clinical notes with varied phrasing, abbreviations, and casing.

The NTE gap

The real story is in the NTE-specific numbers:

GLiNER PII Base — a model specifically designed for PII detection — catches only 29.4% of PHI in NTE segments. NVIDIA's GLiNER variant manages 40.8%. Even the unfine-tuned Privacy Filter only reaches 67.1%. Sentedel v2.1 catches 93.0% of NTE PHI while maintaining 99.7% recall on structured segments.

Approach

How we got here

v2 Experiment
Why not two models?

We tried routing structured segments to our fine-tuned model and NTE text to a clinical de-ID model (obi/deid_roberta_i2b2). It didn't work — the clinical model was trained on lowercase EHR narratives and failed on uppercase EDI-style NTE text. The routed pipeline achieved only 68.2% NTE recall vs 99.3% from the structural model alone.

Key Insight
Single model wins

A single model fine-tuned on both structured and NTE-containing data outperforms a specialized dual-model architecture for this task. Simpler was better.

The data problem

The 99.3% NTE recall in v2 was misleading — those NTE segments were generated from 20 handcrafted templates, predictable enough that a model trained on structural patterns could catch them without understanding clinical prose. We needed more diverse, realistic NTE content to truly test generalization.

LLM-generated NTE templates

v2.1 uses Qwen 2.5 7B-Instruct (4-bit quantized) to generate diverse NTE note templates. The LLM receives a system prompt instructing it to produce clinical notes using placeholder tokens — <<PATIENT_NAME>>, <<MEMBER_ID>>, <<PATIENT_DOB>> — with varied styles, abbreviations, and clinical contexts.

During synthetic EDI generation, the placeholders get replaced with Faker-generated PHI values, giving exact character-level labels through offset tracking during substitution. The LLM decides structure and style; the generator controls the PHI values.

Result

217 unique NTE templates. NTE recall dropped from 99.3% (handcrafted templates) to 93.0% (LLM-generated) — proving the model is now being genuinely tested against diverse clinical language.

Technical details

Base model:       openai/privacy-filter (1.5B params, 50M active, MoE)
Training data:    5,000 synthetic 837P transactions (35% with NTE segments)
NTE templates:    217 unique (137 LLM-generated + 80 handcrafted fallback)
Training split:   4,200 train / 300 val / 500 test
Label strategy:   S-tag alignment into existing PII categories
Training:         3 epochs, lr=3e-4, bf16, dynamic padding, batch 4 (eff. 16)
Evaluation:       Multi-threshold (strict/relaxed), structured vs NTE breakdown
